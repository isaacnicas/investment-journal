Where great investments actually come from — before anyone agrees they were obvious.

Lessons from history's greatest investors, distilled into practical wisdom.

Buy low, sell high. Great advice, if you also have a time machine.

It's the first thing anyone learns about investing, and the last thing anyone actually masters. Nobody tells you how to know what "low" is until after it's already gone "high." So if that's not a strategy, what is?

Imagine Buffett, Cathie Wood, Peter Lynch, Jim Simons, Howard Marks, and Stanley Druckenmiller at the same dinner table. Within five minutes they'd disagree about almost everything.

Six wildly different toolkits — and yet all six are legendary. That's not a coincidence to shrug at. It's a clue. Underneath the different styles, every one of them is a professional noticer of change. This series is built on doing exactly what Buffett did, just with six heroes instead of one: stitching their different mechanisms into one usable system. Not chasing a single guru's playbook, but building an all-star team out of investors who'd probably never agree to be on the same team. Nobody passes the ball. Everybody still wins.

Stage One

Why Plant the Tree at All

Before any of this, there's a simpler question: why invest at all? It seems obvious until you watch how many people never start, or start ten years later than they should have.

"The best time to plant a tree was 20 years ago. The second best time is now."

Chinese proverb

That's the generational case. There's a more immediate one sitting right under it: cash that just sits there quietly loses ground every year, whether you notice or not.

Illustrative, at a 3% average annual inflation rate. The bills in the drawer don't change. What they buy does.

That's the quiet argument for investing at all: doing nothing isn't neutral, it's a slowly losing position. But that just establishes the floor. It doesn't answer the harder question — the one Howard Marks essentially poses in his own work.

The Marks Challenge

Tens of thousands of analysts and algorithms are pricing these same markets for a living, every day. Marks has put the logic plainly: if enough rational, well-informed people are evaluating an asset, its price shouldn't drift far from what it's worth. Sit with two questions before reading any further.

If markets are this efficient, and professionals with better resources than you are already in them — why bother investing at all? And if you do: what's actually your edge? If you can't answer that second one honestly, you aren't investing — you're just donating to Wall Street.

Here's why the first question doesn't end the conversation. Markets aren't as efficient as the theory wants, and two different investors spent careers proving it in two different ways.

The Counter-Case

George Soros built a fortune on the simple observation that prices don't just reflect reality — they're also shaped by what investors feel and believe, which then changes the reality those beliefs were supposed to be reflecting. Fear and herd behavior aren't noise around the price. Sometimes they're the whole story.

Even Eugene Fama's own data turned against pure efficiency — and Fama (the father of the Efficient Market Hypothesis) isn't exactly a hostile witness here. Stocks that have been rising tend to keep rising for months at a time, a pattern called momentum, and it's persisted for decades despite everyone knowing about it. Fama himself once called it the biggest embarrassment to his own theory.

Momentum is just one entry in a longer list. Researchers have identified a whole family of these statistical patterns, quietly called "factors," that quantitative funds now build entire strategies around — more on that in a future issue.

So the market is efficient enough that lazy analysis won't beat it, and inefficient enough that careful work still can. That's not a loophole. That's the entire reason this publication exists.

Your edge isn't beating the market at its own best-covered game. It's choosing where the game is still open — and staying honest about why you, specifically, are looking there.

Stage Two

Where Ideas Actually Come From

Most people search for tips. A friend mentions a ticker, a headline screams a number, and the search ends before it started. The trouble is a tip is a conclusion someone else already reached — by the time it gets to you, a few thousand other people have it too.

The gold line is what a sharp investor is watching. The grey one is what everyone else calls "the market."

The Scoreboard Problem

Most investors are watching the scoreboard. The best investors are watching the game.

The scoreboard only updates after something's already happened on the field — and by the time it changes, everyone in the stadium knows. The game is where the information actually lives, and almost nobody in the stands is watching it.

Great investors search for developments instead of tips. Change tends to show up in a handful of recognizable places — call them the eight sources.

1.

Future Change — Cathie Wood⌄

Wood's whole career is one mission: find the technologies disruptive enough to change how the world works, then own them years before that's obvious — AI, robotics, gene sequencing, energy storage, blockchain. The advantage is real. The danger is that being early and being wrong look identical for years.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice, her method is a question, not a hunch: why hasn't this broken through yet? Name the actual blocker — humanoid robots are capped by battery life and hard-to-scale parts, solar EVs are capped by a roof simply being too small to capture enough sun — then ask what has to go right, and who's closest to fixing exactly that.
2.

Consumer Change — Peter Lynch⌄

New habits, products suddenly everywhere, before Wall Street looks up from its spreadsheets. Think AirPods showing up in every commute years before analysts upgraded Apple's earnings outlook because of them. The trap: noticing a trend isn't understanding the business behind it.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: notice the habit, then go find the unglamorous supplier or platform actually capturing the economics of it.
3.

Business Change — Carl Icahn⌄

Revenue accelerating, margins expanding, a turnaround quietly working before the stock believes it. Icahn didn't wait for that turnaround to happen on its own — he bought a large stake in companies with good potential but poor management, then forced the change himself: new leadership, sold-off assets, restructured costs. Unglamorous, spreadsheet-shaped change, engineered rather than spotted.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: read the last three quarters back to back. Acceleration shows up in the trend long before it shows up in the headline — and if it isn't showing up yet, ask whether current management is even capable of making it happen.
4.

Bottleneck Change — Peter Thiel⌄

Don't bet on who wins the race. Find the chokepoint everyone in the race has to pass through regardless of who wins. Thiel built his case for monopoly-style businesses on exactly this: the hidden constraint that quietly decides who actually captures the value.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: when an industry is racing toward something, ask what every single competitor needs and can't make themselves. The supplier of that thing often makes more money than the racers.
5.

Industry Change — John Templeton⌄

New regulation, disruption, consolidation — the rules of an entire industry shifting while most players still play by the old ones. Templeton built a decades-long career on a blunter version of the same idea: buy whole industries, even whole nations, at the point of maximum pessimism, when almost everyone else has already given up on the sector entirely.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: don't ask which company in a hated industry is healthiest. Ask whether the whole sector is now priced for a disaster that isn't actually coming — that's where the entire industry reprices at once, not just one stock.
6.

Market Perception Change — Howard Marks⌄

Nothing about the business changes — only the story the market tells itself does. A solid company misses one quarter, the headlines call it a crisis, and the price falls further than the actual facts justify.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: when the headline is scarier than the 10-K, that gap is the opportunity.
7.

Macro Change — Stanley Druckenmiller⌄

Rates, currencies, commodity cycles — the slow tectonic shifts that quietly reprice entire asset classes.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: you don't need to predict the Fed. You just need to notice when the tide has already turned.
8.

Data-Detected Change — Jim Simons⌄

Looks at the other seven and says prove it. Real statistical patterns, invisible to the eye, beat a good story every time.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: ask if your "insight" has happened before. If you can't check, you don't have a pattern — you have a hope.

None of these investors are predicting anything. They're standing closer to where the change is actually happening.

Stage Three

So What Counts as "Low"?

Back to the question from the top: nobody tells you how to know what "low" is until after it's already gone "high." Here's the honest answer — you don't know it from the price alone. You know it by comparing the price to two other things: what similar businesses cost, and how defensible this one actually is.

Start with the boring part. A P/E ratio — price divided by earnings — is just a translator. It converts "the stock costs $90" into "you're paying 19 times what the company earns per share each year," which is the number that's actually comparable across companies. A $90 stock and a $9 stock can be priced identically once you do this; a $90 stock can also be the cheaper one. Price alone tells you nothing. The multiple tells you something.

Illustrative. Same industry, same growth ballpark — and the gold bar costs less for every dollar of earnings.

That gap is interesting. It is not, on its own, a reason to buy. A lower multiple sometimes means "cheap." It sometimes means "cheap for a reason" — worse management, a shrinking market, a balance sheet held together with tape. The only way to tell the two apart is the question underneath all of this: why does this business get to keep charging what it charges, and for how long?

The Moat Check
Switching cost
Brand / trust
Scale advantage
Network effect

A moat isn't one big wall — it's usually two or three of these, working together, that make it expensive for a competitor to take the customer away.

Put the two together and the cheap-multiple question gets an actual answer. A low P/E next to a real moat is a genuine signal — the market is mispricing a business that can defend its margins for years. A low P/E next to a weak moat is usually just an accurate price on a business that's about to get squeezed. Same number, opposite conclusion. The multiple tells you where to look. The moat tells you what you're allowed to conclude once you're there.

That's the actual strategy hiding behind "buy low, sell high": not predicting the bottom, but pricing a business honestly against its peers and its defenses, then buying when the market hasn't bothered to do the same homework.

Stage Four

Getting In: Gut, Math, or Both

An idea is not an investment — it's an invitation to investigate. Once it survives that, the next question is sizing.

"An undersized position in a high-conviction idea is a wasted insight."

The spirit of Stanley Druckenmiller's sizing philosophy

If you've done the work and you're right, timidity is its own mistake. The opposite pole is the Kelly Criterion — pure math, sizing the bet off your edge, no gut involved. Most disciplined investors live in the middle: starter position, then scale up once the thesis confirms. You earn the right to go big by being right first.

GutMath
The starter position

Small enough that being wrong doesn't hurt, sized just large enough to earn your attention. You scale up only once the thesis confirms — conviction, time-delayed and risk-gated.

The Munger Inversion

Before sizing anything, don't ask why this works. Ask what would make it a disaster. If you can't answer that, you understand the pitch, not the investment.

Stage Five

Holding On, and Letting Go

Passive, hands-off investing beats most active strategies long-term — that's not controversial, it's one of the best-documented facts in finance. Drag the slider below and watch what "long-term" actually costs an active stock-picker.

1 year

Based on S&P's SPIVA scorecards. The longer you measure, the worse stock-picking looks — for professionals being paid to do it full-time.

That single fact does most of the heavy lifting in this section. If trained, well-resourced professionals mostly lose to a default index over any meaningful stretch, the bar for justifying your own active decisions is high. So why discuss active decisions at all?

The other 90 are why "just hold an index fund" is the right default — and why the few moments you do act deserve real scrutiny.

Those rare, real moments are exactly what a passive approach is designed to ignore: a thesis breaking, a position becoming dangerously oversized through pure appreciation, a genuinely better opportunity replacing a mediocre one. None of those are about price going up or down. They're about whether the original reason you bought still holds.

When to Actually Sell

Real Reasons

The thesis broke. You bought because of a specific, statable reason — accelerating revenue, a turnaround, a misunderstood story. If that reason is no longer true, the position has nothing left holding it up except your reluctance to admit it.

The position outgrew its risk budget. A stock that doubled or tripled isn't the same size decision it was at purchase. Trimming back to your original sizing isn't losing faith — it's the position-sizing discipline from Stage Four, applied after the fact instead of before.

Something better showed up. Capital is finite. A mediocre holding competing against a stronger new idea isn't entitled to keep its seat just because it's already there.

Reasons That Feel Real But Aren't

The price went down and it stings. The price went up and you want to "lock in" the win. You're bored. Someone on a podcast disagreed with you. None of these say anything about whether the original thesis is still true — they're just feelings wearing a thesis costume.

The thesis breaking and the price moving are two different events, and they rarely happen on the same day. Most people wait for the second one before acting on the first — which means by the time selling feels obvious, you've already given away the head start that noticing the thesis break should have bought you.

"If you aren't willing to own a stock for ten years, don't own it for ten minutes."

Warren Buffett

Read literally, this sounds like a timeframe rule. Read correctly, it's the holding-side mirror of the selling framework above: a ten-year holding period is only sane for a business whose thesis you'd still believe in year ten. Most positions don't qualify, and pretending they do is how mediocre holdings calcify into "I'll just wait for it to come back."

Which is exactly backwards from what the math wants — cut losses fast, let winners run — advice everyone can recite and almost no one follows. People sell winners early to lock in a good feeling and hold losers forever waiting for vindication. The disposition effect has a name precisely because it's nearly universal, not because it's rare.

Here's the inversion worth sitting with: you wouldn't buy your worst-performing position today at today's price, knowing what you now know. If that's true, holding it isn't a decision — it's just the absence of one.

Buffett claims he's never made a decision based on emotion. Superhuman discipline, or a flattering memory — doesn't matter, the aspiration's the point. Every stage above is really the same fight: narrative bias hunting ideas, overconfidence or underconfidence sizing them, loss aversion holding them, recency bias deciding when the wave's already crested.

Catching a wave early — Wood buying BTC and NVDA years before either was consensus — isn't mystical. It's a belief, backed by research, sized with conviction once the thesis firmed up, held through volatility because nothing broke. Four stages, working at once.

Key Takeaways

  • Tips are conclusions other people already reached. Developments are raw material nobody's finished using yet.
  • Change shows up in recognizable places: the future, the consumer, the business itself, the industry, market perception, the macro backdrop, or the data.
  • An idea is not an investment. It's an invitation to go check.
  • Munger's inversion — what would make this a disaster? — is the fastest way to tell if you understand something or just like it.
  • Buying right and holding right are different skills, and most damage happens in the gap between them.
Action Step — This Week

Pick one of the eight sources above and find a single piece of concrete evidence of it in the real world — not a stock, not a ticker, just one thing that's shifted and hasn't fully shown up in a price yet. Write it down. That sentence is the seed of every great investment anyone has ever made.
