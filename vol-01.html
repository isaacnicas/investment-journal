Where great investments actually come from — before anyone agrees they were obvious.

Lessons from history's greatest investors, distilled into practical wisdom.

Buy low, sell high. Great advice, if you also have a time machine.

It's the first thing anyone learns about investing, and the last thing anyone actually masters. Nobody tells you how to know what "low" is until after it's already gone "high." And here's the part that makes it worse: even when you do find good advice, the next legendary investor you read will often tell you the exact opposite, with an equally impressive track record to back it up.

Imagine Buffett, Cathie Wood, Peter Lynch, Jim Simons, Howard Marks, and Stanley Druckenmiller at the same dinner table. Within five minutes they'd disagree about almost everything.

Buffett himself has a name for what this dinner table actually is.

"The best thing I did was to choose the right heroes."

Warren Buffett

That's what this series is built on doing, just with six heroes instead of one. But here's the honest question worth asking before we go any further: if they're all legendary, why do they look nothing alike? It's because none of them are playing the same game. Each one is a professional noticer of change, optimizing for a completely different variable, and once you actually see the shape of that, on the same axes, at the same time, the rest of this volume gets a lot easier to read.

Tap a name. Watch the shape point somewhere completely different — that's the whole argument.

Comparing Buffett's patience to Druckenmiller's asymmetry is like asking whether a hammer is better than a tape measure — the question only makes sense once you know what you're building. You can't max out all six variables at once, but you can choose, deliberately, which one matters most for the decision in front of you, and borrow the right tool for that job instead of running everything through the same single lens.

Buffett's approach, applied to a Wood-style decision, will tell you to wait for cash flows that don't exist yet. Wood's approach, applied to a Buffett-style decision, will tell you to chase volatility a stable business was never built to provide. Neither is wrong. They're just answering different questions.

Stage One

Why Plant the Tree at All

Before any of this, there's a simpler question: why invest at all? It seems obvious until you watch how many people never start, or start ten years later than they should have.

"The best time to plant a tree was 20 years ago. The second best time is now."

Chinese proverb

That's the generational case. There's a more immediate one sitting right under it: cash that just sits there quietly loses ground every year, whether you notice or not.

Illustrative, at a 3% average annual inflation rate. The bills in the drawer don't change. What they buy does.

That's the quiet argument for investing at all: doing nothing isn't neutral, it's a slowly losing position. But that just establishes the floor. It doesn't answer the harder question — the one Howard Marks essentially poses in his own work.

The Marks Challenge

Tens of thousands of analysts and algorithms are pricing these same markets for a living, every day. Marks has put the logic plainly: if enough rational, well-informed people are evaluating an asset, its price shouldn't drift far from what it's worth. Sit with two questions before reading any further.

If markets are this efficient, and professionals with better resources than you are already in them — why bother investing at all? And if you do: what's actually your edge? If you can't answer that second one honestly, you aren't investing — you're just donating to Wall Street.

Here's why the first question doesn't end the conversation. Markets aren't as efficient as the theory wants, and two different investors spent careers proving it in two different ways.

The Counter-Case

George Soros built a fortune on the simple observation that prices don't just reflect reality — they're also shaped by what investors feel and believe, which then changes the reality those beliefs were supposed to be reflecting. Fear and herd behavior aren't noise around the price. Sometimes they're the whole story.

Even Eugene Fama's own data turned against pure efficiency — and Fama (the father of the Efficient Market Hypothesis) isn't exactly a hostile witness here. Stocks that have been rising tend to keep rising for months at a time, a pattern called momentum, and it's persisted for decades despite everyone knowing about it. Fama himself once called it the biggest embarrassment to his own theory.

Momentum is just one entry in a longer list. Researchers have identified a whole family of these statistical patterns, quietly called "factors," that quantitative funds now build entire strategies around — more on that in a future issue.

So the market is efficient enough that lazy analysis won't beat it, and inefficient enough that careful work still can. That's not a loophole. That's the entire reason this publication exists.

Your edge isn't beating the market at its own best-covered game. It's choosing where the game is still open — and staying honest about why you, specifically, are looking there.

Stage Two

Where Ideas Actually Come From

So where is the game still open? Not in the places everyone else is already looking. Most people search for tips instead. A friend mentions a ticker, a headline screams a number, and the search ends before it started. The trouble is a tip is a conclusion someone else already reached — by the time it gets to you, a few thousand other people have it too.

The gold line is what a sharp investor is watching. The grey one is what everyone else calls "the market."

The Scoreboard Problem

Most investors are watching the scoreboard. The best investors are watching the game.

The scoreboard only updates after something's already happened on the field — and by the time it changes, everyone in the stadium knows. The game is where the information actually lives, and almost nobody in the stands is watching it.

Great investors search for developments instead of tips. Change tends to show up in a handful of recognizable places — call them the eight sources.

1.

Future Change — Cathie Wood⌄

Wood's whole career is one mission: find the technologies disruptive enough to change how the world works, then own them years before that's obvious. The advantage is real. The danger is that being early and being wrong look identical for years.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice, her method is a question, not a hunch: take today's version of a technology and rigorously research what it could plausibly look like in 20 years, not as a guess, but as a forecast built on the actual trajectory of cost, capability, and adoption. The most important question isn't "is this real?" It's "why is this still 5, 10, or 20 years away?" Name the actual blocker. Then ask who's actively working on resolving exactly that blocker.
2.

Consumer Change — Peter Lynch⌄

New habits, products suddenly everywhere, before Wall Street looks up from its spreadsheets. Think AirPods showing up in every commute years before analysts upgraded Apple's earnings outlook because of them. The trap: noticing a trend isn't understanding the business behind it.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: notice the habit, then go find the unglamorous supplier or platform actually capturing the economics of it.
3.

Business Change — Carl Icahn⌄

"The news optimizes for attention. Data optimizes for truth." That's the gap Icahn built a career exploiting — he never waited for headlines to confirm a turnaround was happening, because by the time they do, the price has already moved. He bought large stakes in companies with good potential but poor management, then forced the change himself: new leadership, sold-off assets, restructured costs. Unglamorous, spreadsheet-shaped change, engineered rather than spotted.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: read the last three quarters back to back. Acceleration shows up in the trend long before it shows up in the headline — and if it isn't showing up yet, ask whether current management is even capable of making it happen.
4.

Bottleneck Change — Peter Thiel⌄

Don't bet on who wins the race. Find the chokepoint everyone in the race has to pass through regardless of who wins. Thiel built his case for monopoly-style businesses on exactly this: the hidden constraint that quietly decides who actually captures the value.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: when an industry is racing toward something, ask what every single competitor needs and can't make themselves. The supplier of that thing often makes more money than the racers.
5.

Industry Change — John Templeton⌄

New regulation, disruption, consolidation — the rules of an entire industry shifting while most players still play by the old ones. Templeton built a decades-long career on a blunter version of the same idea: buy whole industries, even whole nations, at the point of maximum pessimism, when almost everyone else has already given up on the sector entirely.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: don't ask which company in a hated industry is healthiest. Ask whether the whole sector is now priced for a disaster that isn't actually coming — that's where the entire industry reprices at once, not just one stock.
6.

Market Perception Change — Howard Marks⌄

Nothing about the business changes — only the story the market tells itself does. A solid company misses one quarter, the crowd decides it's a crisis, and the price falls further than the actual deterioration justifies. The business is the same. The mood around it isn't.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: ask whether the price moved because something about the business actually broke, or because everyone simultaneously decided to feel worse about the same set of facts. Sentiment swings faster than fundamentals ever do — that gap is the opportunity.
7.

Macro Change — Stanley Druckenmiller⌄

Rates, currencies, commodity cycles — the slow tectonic shifts that quietly reprice entire asset classes.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice: you don't need to predict the Fed. You just need to notice when the tide has already turned.
8.

Data-Detected Change — Jim Simons⌄

Looks at the other seven and says prove it. Simons hunts for change too, he just finds it in a different place: not in a story or a headline, but as an anomaly buried in the data, a pattern breaking from its usual behavior in a way that's statistically real, whether or not anyone can explain why. The discipline is in how he confirms it. Real statistical patterns repeat. A coincidence doesn't. Simons built Renaissance Technologies on refusing to act until something has shown up again and again, enough times that it stops being a story and starts being a fact.

Tap to expand the practice step
In practice, you don't need a research team to borrow the spirit of this. Best practice for now: let data confirm conviction, not replace it. Before acting on a thesis, check it against something other than your own gut. Free screeners like Finviz or Yahoo Finance's screener let you filter for the actual numbers behind a story, revenue growth, margin trends, insider buying, instead of taking a headline's word for it. TradingView is a good next step once you want to see how a price has actually behaved over time, not just where it sits today. None of this requires code or a finance degree, just the habit of checking the data before trusting the narrative. Once that feels comfortable, there's a deeper layer: quantitative factor modeling, momentum, size, volatility, and simulation-based approaches that funds like Renaissance build entire strategies around. You don't need any of that yet. We'll get into it properly in a future volume.

Eight different ways of noticing change, eight different starting points. With that mental toolkit in hand, we can move on to the part that actually matters: the underlying truths that let these different approaches sync into one usable system.

Stage Three

Why They Actually Win

Every one of these six has biases, blind spots, and at least one famously bad call — Buffett spent years skeptical of tech before Apple became the single most profitable position Berkshire ever held. So if none of them are oracles, what's actually doing the work?

Not genius. Morgan Housel borrows a line from Napoleon in The Psychology of Money that answers it better than we could — "a military genius is the man who can do the average thing when all those around him are going crazy." Swap "military" for "investing," and below is exactly what that looks like for each of them.

WARREN BUFFETT
The Truth: Most People Buy and Sell at the Wrong Time
Temperament beats timing

Two mistakes, same root cause. On the way down, loss aversion (people feel a loss roughly twice as intensely as an equivalent gain) pushes investors to sell into a drawdown instead of through one. On the way up, the opposite pole, FOMO, pushes them to grab a falling knife's cheerful cousin: chasing a price that's already run, just because everyone else is buying. Buffett's entire edge is refusing to let either feeling drive the decision. As he's put it plainly, you have to be prepared to watch a stock fall 50% or more and still be comfortable holding it, the same way a farmer doesn't check his land's resale value every morning. His other quiet trick: sometimes the best decision you can make is not to make one. You don't need to act on every opportunity, and you don't need more than a handful of genuinely good businesses to reach your goals.

The step: before buying anything, write down the drawdown you'd need to sit through without flinching. If you can't name a number, you don't know your own temperament yet, and that's worth finding out before your money does.

STANLEY DRUCKENMILLER
The Truth: Being Right Often Isn't the Same as Making Money
Asymmetry beats accuracy

Most people, and most investing advice, quietly optimize for win rate, being right as often as possible. Druckenmiller has said directly that being right or wrong matters less than how much you make when you're right and how much you lose when you're wrong. You can be correct 40% of the time and still come out far ahead, if your wins are sized for it and your losses are capped.

The step: stop scoring yourself on how often you're right. Score yourself on the ratio between your average win and your average loss instead.

CATHIE WOOD
The Truth: Humans Can't Intuitively Picture Exponential Growth
Early beats accurate

This one has a name: exponential growth bias, the well-documented tendency to mentally flatten a doubling curve into a straight line, confirmed in studies even among highly numerate people. It's why most observers miss disruptive technologies until they're already mainstream. Wood's process is built specifically to fight this, tracking cost-decline curves (the same logic behind Wright's Law) to estimate when an emerging technology's price will fall enough to unlock the next wave of demand, before the growth is obvious to anyone glancing at a chart.

The step: when a trend looks "too early to matter," check whether its underlying cost or adoption curve is doubling on a schedule. If it is, your gut is almost certainly underestimating it.

PETER LYNCH
The Truth: Wall Street Is Structurally Far From the Ground
Proximity beats credentials

Professional analysts mostly encounter a business through spreadsheets and earnings calls, not the store itself. Lynch's edge wasn't a sharper model, it was literal physical proximity: noticing a product everyone around him was suddenly buying, long before that showed up in anyone's research note. He was explicit that ordinary investors have a real structural advantage here, not a sentimental one.

The step: the things you notice in your own daily life, genuinely, repeatedly, are data. Write them down before you forget you noticed them at all.

HOWARD MARKS
The Truth: Price and Value Are Not the Same Number
Value beats price

It sounds almost too obvious to state, until you watch how often "it's down a lot" gets treated as a reason to buy, all by itself. Marks' whole career is built on the distinction between what something costs and what it's worth, and on the discipline to ask which one moved when a price changes. A falling price tells you nothing about value on its own. It only tells you that the crowd's opinion shifted.

The step: every time a price moves sharply, ask explicitly whether the business changed or only the opinion of it did. Those call for opposite reactions.

JIM SIMONS
The Truth: A Good Story Is Not the Same as a Repeating Pattern
Repetition beats narrative

Humans are wired to find causal stories compelling, even in pure noise, that's the entire premise of narrative bias. Simons built Renaissance Technologies on the opposite instinct: ignore the story entirely, and trust only what shows up again and again in the data, regardless of whether anyone can explain why. It's the least romantic edge on this list, and one of the most quietly devastating.

The step: when you catch yourself explaining why a trade should work, ask separately whether you've actually seen it work before, more than once, under different conditions.

Six different facts. None of them require six different talents, just six different decisions to take one plain truth seriously and act on it past the point where it stopped feeling clever. That's the actual takeaway: you don't need to be a genius to use any single one of these. You need to actually apply it, consistently, on the days it's inconvenient.

And here, finally, is why the dinner table doesn't end in a fistfight. They're not competing for the same insight. Buffett isn't trying to out-asymmetry Druckenmiller, and Simons isn't trying to out-proximity Lynch. Each one has quietly claimed a different corner of the same truth, and none of those corners overlap enough to fight over. Six people, six non-overlapping blind spots fixed. That's not a coincidence. That's the actual reason this dinner table works: nobody there needs to be wrong for anybody else to be right.

Stage Four

So What Counts as "Low"?

Let's be honest about something first: if you're not a math person, investing has an uncomfortable habit of pretending that's optional. For the average investor, on a normal day, you do not buy 47 watermelons, calculate a 17% tip in your head, or split a dinner bill three ways without at least one person pulling out their phone, squinting at the screen like they're decoding ancient runes. And then you're handed a stock ticker and somehow expected to instantly know what "cheap" means. But we're in luck, because you don't need differential equations to start a successful investing journey. You need about four ideas, explained simply, and the discipline to actually use them.

Back to the question from the top: nobody tells you how to know what "low" is until after it's already gone "high." Here's the honest answer — you don't know it from the price alone. You know it by comparing the price to what similar businesses cost, how defensible the business actually is, and how much room you've left yourself for being wrong. Four blocks, four ideas, no math degree required.

P/E Ratio
Price ÷ Earnings per share

A translator, nothing more. It converts "the stock costs $90" into "you're paying 19 times what the company earns per share each year," the number that's actually comparable across companies. A $90 stock and a $9 stock can be priced identically once you do this; a $90 stock can also be the cheaper one.

Best for: a fast first comparison between similar, profitable companies in the same industry.

PEG Ratio
P/E ÷ expected earnings growth rate

P/E's correction for growth-blindness. A P/E of 30 on a company growing 30% a year produces a PEG of 1.0, traditionally read as fair value. That same P/E of 30 on a company growing only 10% a year produces a PEG of 3.0, a much pricier story wearing the exact same multiple.

Best for: comparing a fast grower against a slow one without being fooled by the sticker price.

Margin of Safety
(Estimated value − Price) ÷ Estimated value

Buffett's own definition is the simplest one: buying a dollar of value for fifty cents. You estimate what a business is actually worth, then only buy if the price sits well below that, usually 20 to 30% below, so your own mistakes have room to be wrong in without costing you the principal.

Best for: literally every purchase, as the safety net underneath whichever other multiple you used.

Price-to-Book
Price ÷ Net assets per share

Instead of comparing price to profit, this compares price to what the company would be worth if it sold everything it owns and paid off every debt today. A P/B under 1 means you're paying less than the company's own assets are stated to be worth on paper.

Best for: asset-heavy businesses, banks, insurers, manufacturers, where physical assets and loans dominate the balance sheet. Far less useful for software or brand-driven companies, where most of the real value never shows up on the balance sheet at all.

That gap is interesting. It is not, on its own, a reason to buy. A lower multiple sometimes means "cheap." It sometimes means "cheap for a reason," what investors call a value trap: shrinking revenue, a balance sheet held together with debt, a business correctly priced for trouble rather than mistakenly overlooked. The only way to tell the two apart is the question underneath all of this: why does this business get to keep charging what it charges, and for how long?

The Moat Check
Switching cost
Brand / trust
Scale advantage
Network effect

A moat isn't one big wall — it's usually two or three of these, working together, that make it expensive for a competitor to take the customer away.

Put the two together and the cheap-multiple question gets an actual answer. A low P/E next to a real moat is a genuine signal — the market is mispricing a business that can defend its margins for years. A low P/E next to a weak moat is usually just an accurate price on a business that's about to get squeezed. Same number, opposite conclusion. The multiple tells you where to look. The moat tells you what you're allowed to conclude once you're there. And the margin of safety is what protects you on the days you got that conclusion wrong anyway.

One last thing worth saying plainly: "buy low, sell high" is one perfectly good strategy. It is not the only one. Sometimes the better trade is buy high, sell higher, riding a price that's already moving rather than waiting for a discount that never comes. That's a different game with different rules, momentum and trend-following among them, and we'll get into that toolkit properly in a future issue.

Stage Five

Getting In: Gut, Math, or Both

An idea is not an investment — it's an invitation to investigate. Once it survives that, the next question is sizing.

"An undersized position in a high-conviction idea is a wasted insight."

The spirit of Stanley Druckenmiller's sizing philosophy

If you've done the work and you're right, timidity is its own mistake. But going to the opposite extreme — betting everything on a high-conviction idea — runs into a problem that mathematics worked out long before most investors thought to ask.

In the 1960s, Ed Thorp — mathematician, blackjack player, and the founder of what many consider the first true quantitative hedge fund — took a formula derived by Bell Labs scientist John Kelly and applied it directly to financial markets. The Kelly Criterion asks a simple question: given your edge and the odds, what fraction of your capital should you bet? The answer is precise: bet size equals edge divided by odds. Bet more than this fraction and your long-run growth rate actually falls, even with a genuine edge. Bet much more and you risk ruin. The mathematics doesn't care how confident you feel.

"The Kelly formula tells you how to get rich. The half-Kelly tells you how to get rich without the stress."

Ed Thorp, A Man for All Markets

Most disciplined investors live somewhere between the Druckenmiller end — size matches conviction — and the Kelly end — size matches math. The practical middle ground is a starter position that earns your attention, scaled up only once the thesis confirms. You earn the right to go big by being right first. Half-Kelly is the professional default for exactly the reason Thorp described: the full Kelly maximizes long-run growth in theory, but the variance is brutal. Half-Kelly gives up a small amount of growth in exchange for a dramatically smoother ride.

GutMath
The starter position

Small enough that being wrong doesn't hurt, sized just large enough to earn your attention. You scale up only once the thesis confirms — conviction, time-delayed and risk-gated.

The Munger Inversion

Before sizing anything, don't ask why this works. Ask what would make it a disaster. If you can't answer that, you understand the pitch, not the investment.

Stage Six

Holding On, and Letting Go

Passive, hands-off investing beats most active strategies long-term — that's not controversial, it's one of the best-documented facts in finance. Drag the slider below and watch what "long-term" actually costs an active stock-picker.

1 year

Based on S&P's SPIVA scorecards. The longer you measure, the worse stock-picking looks — for professionals being paid to do it full-time.

That single fact does most of the heavy lifting in this section. If trained, well-resourced professionals mostly lose to a default index over any meaningful stretch, the bar for justifying your own active decisions is high. So why discuss active decisions at all?

The other 90 are why "just hold an index fund" is the right default — and why the few moments you do act deserve real scrutiny.

Those rare, real moments are exactly what a passive approach is designed to ignore: a thesis breaking, a position becoming dangerously oversized through pure appreciation, a genuinely better opportunity replacing a mediocre one. None of those are about price going up or down. They're about whether the original reason you bought still holds.

When to Actually Sell

Real Reasons

The thesis broke. You bought because of a specific, statable reason — accelerating revenue, a turnaround, a misunderstood story. If that reason is no longer true, the position has nothing left holding it up except your reluctance to admit it.

The position outgrew its risk budget. A stock that doubled or tripled isn't the same size decision it was at purchase. Trimming back to your original sizing isn't losing faith — it's the position-sizing discipline from Stage Five, applied after the fact instead of before.

Something better showed up. Capital is finite. A mediocre holding competing against a stronger new idea isn't entitled to keep its seat just because it's already there.

Reasons That Feel Real But Aren't

The price went down and it stings. The price went up and you want to "lock in" the win. You're bored. Someone on a podcast disagreed with you. None of these say anything about whether the original thesis is still true — they're just feelings wearing a thesis costume.

The thesis breaking and the price moving are two different events, and they rarely happen on the same day. Most people wait for the second one before acting on the first — which means by the time selling feels obvious, you've already given away the head start that noticing the thesis break should have bought you.

"If you aren't willing to own a stock for ten years, don't own it for ten minutes."

Warren Buffett

Read literally, this sounds like a timeframe rule. Read correctly, it's the holding-side mirror of the selling framework above: a ten-year holding period is only sane for a business whose thesis you'd still believe in year ten. Most positions don't qualify, and pretending they do is how mediocre holdings calcify into "I'll just wait for it to come back."

Which is exactly backwards from what the math wants — cut losses fast, let winners run — advice everyone can recite and almost no one follows. People sell winners early to lock in a good feeling and hold losers forever waiting for vindication. The disposition effect has a name precisely because it's nearly universal, not because it's rare.

Here's the inversion worth sitting with: you wouldn't buy your worst-performing position today at today's price, knowing what you now know. If that's true, holding it isn't a decision — it's just the absence of one.

Before the close, the five lines worth actually remembering:

Key Takeaways

  • Tips are conclusions other people already reached. Developments are raw material nobody's finished using yet.
  • Change shows up in recognizable places: the future, the consumer, the business itself, the industry, market perception, the macro backdrop, or the data.
  • An idea is not an investment. It's an invitation to go check.
  • Munger's inversion — what would make this a disaster? — is the fastest way to tell if you understand something or just like it.
  • Buying right and holding right are different skills, and most damage happens in the gap between them.
Action Step — This Week

Pick one of the eight sources above and find a single piece of concrete evidence of it in the real world — not a stock, not a ticker, just one thing that's shifted and hasn't fully shown up in a price yet. Write it down. That sentence is the seed of every great investment anyone has ever made.
